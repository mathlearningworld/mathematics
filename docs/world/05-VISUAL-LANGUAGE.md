# 05 — Visual Language

## Visual objective

The world should feel handcrafted, warm, readable, and worthy of exploration. Visual beauty must support orientation, curiosity, emotional safety, and gameplay comprehension.

## Composition hierarchy

Every production scene should establish:

1. a clear primary landmark;
2. a readable player route;
3. supporting terrain masses;
4. foreground framing without obstruction;
5. controlled detail density;
6. atmospheric depth;
7. a memorable visual rhythm.

Asset quantity must never substitute for composition.

## Shape language

- Settled and welcoming areas favor grounded, rounded, stable forms.
- Discovery areas may use layered, partially concealed, or directional forms.
- Places of understanding should feel ordered without becoming sterile.
- Dangerous or unresolved spaces may use imbalance and compression, but should remain legible.

## Material language

Materials should communicate use, age, climate, and craft. Avoid generic surfaces disconnected from the region's life.

Core families:

- warm timber with visible construction logic;
- stone shaped by terrain and human use;
- water as movement, direction, and connection;
- vegetation grouped by ecosystem rather than scattered decoration;
- metal and crystal used selectively as focal accents.

## Color doctrine

Color is emotional geography.

- Builder's Valley: warm earth, living green, clear water, sunlit craft.
- Forest of Curiosity: layered greens, cool shadow, small luminous discoveries.
- Crystal Mine: restrained darkness, mineral contrast, navigational glow.
- Home Village: warm social color and varied personal accents.
- Farm Terraces: seasonal rhythm, soil, crop bands, irrigation blue.
- City of Mathematics: coherent civic stone with district-specific accents.
- Temple of Understanding: calm contrast, clarity, and controlled radiance.
- Final Peak: expanded atmosphere, distance, sky, and world-scale coherence.

## Lighting doctrine

Lighting should reveal form and route before spectacle. Key landmarks need readable silhouettes. Interactive areas need sufficient local contrast. Atmospheric effects must not obscure gameplay-critical information.

## Readability boundary

A beautiful scene fails production review when the player cannot quickly identify:

- where they can move;
- what they can interact with;
- what changed;
- what is foreground versus background;
- which object is currently important.

## Character-to-world relationship

Props, architecture, paths, and vegetation must preserve believable scale. Scenes should include recurring human-scale references such as doors, railings, work surfaces, steps, carts, tools, and planted rows.
