## 1. Purpose

This slice tests whether the Phase 16A Cognitive Transformation Graph can represent a real mathematical learning domain from initial mental model through transformation, gameplay evidence, and mastery interpretation.

The purpose is not to define a complete ratio curriculum. The purpose is to prove that the architecture can express:

- a meaningful learning target;
- prerequisite concepts;
- distinct cognitive states;
- stable misconceptions;
- transformations between mental models;
- observable evidence;
- playable world contexts;
- mission candidates;
- mastery decisions that do not depend on completion or score alone.

The validation question is:

> Can one authoritative graph explain what a learner understands, what must change, how the world can create that change, and what evidence is sufficient to recognize it?

---

## 2. Validation Decision

**Result: PASS — ARCHITECTURALLY EXPRESSIVE**

The Phase 16A model can represent ratio and proportional reasoning without reducing the domain to chapter order, isolated exercises, or binary completion.

The slice demonstrates that:

1. concept identity and learning-target identity remain distinct;
2. a learner may occupy multiple meaningful cognitive states around the same concept;
3. misconceptions can be represented as stable models rather than random errors;
4. transformations can specify productive experiences and required contrasts;
5. gameplay actions can produce evidence across accuracy, reasoning, representation, transfer, assistance, and retention;
6. the same target can project into several world systems without those systems redefining learning authority;
7. mastery requires a pattern of evidence rather than one successful mission.

This PASS validates architectural expressiveness only. It does not validate production data structures, scoring thresholds, pedagogy through human trials, or runtime implementation.

---

## 3. Why This Domain Was Selected

Ratio and proportional reasoning is an effective validation domain because it connects elementary number sense to later mathematics.

It depends on or supports:

- quantity comparison;
- multiplication and division;
- fractions;
- equivalent quantities;
- rates;
- percentages;
- scale;
- density and concentration;
- slope;
- linear relationships;
- equations and functions.

It also has strong Builder's Valley projections:

- mixing materials;
- scaling building plans;
- trading goods;
- allocating supplies;
- preparing recipes;
- comparing production efficiency;
- resizing structures;
- coordinating NPC work teams.

A weak architecture would model these as unrelated exercises. The Cognitive Transformation Graph must reveal the common mathematical structure beneath them.

LearningTarget
- targetId: LT-RATIO-001

## 5. Concept Nodes

### 5.1 Quantity Comparison

```text
ConceptNode
- conceptId: CONCEPT-QUANTITY-COMPARISON
- canonicalName: Multiplicative comparison of quantities
- meaning: Two quantities may be compared by how many times one contains another
- representations: objects, bars, number lines, symbolic expressions
- common limitation: learner compares only by additive difference
```

### 5.2 Equivalent Quantities

```text
ConceptNode
- conceptId: CONCEPT-EQUIVALENT-QUANTITIES
- canonicalName: Equivalent relationships under common scaling
- meaning: Multiplying or dividing both related quantities by the same nonzero factor preserves the relationship
- representations: grouped objects, tables, double number lines, equations
- common limitation: learner changes only one quantity
```

### 5.3 Ratio as Ordered Comparison

```text
ConceptNode
- conceptId: CONCEPT-RATIO-ORDERED-COMPARISON
- canonicalName: Ratio as an ordered multiplicative comparison
- meaning: A ratio relates two identified quantities in a specific order
- representations: a:b, a/b, words, tables, diagrams
- common limitation: learner treats ratio values as independent numbers
```

### 5.4 Proportional Relationship

```text
ConceptNode
- conceptId: CONCEPT-PROPORTIONAL-RELATIONSHIP
- canonicalName: Invariant multiplicative relationship
- meaning: Corresponding quantities vary while a multiplicative relationship remains invariant
- representations: ratio tables, double number lines, graphs, equations, world models
- common limitation: learner accepts any paired increase as proportional
```

## 7. Cognitive States

### State R0 — Additive Difference Model

```text
stateId: STATE-RATIO-R0
stateKind: NAIVE_MODEL
description:
  The learner judges relationships primarily by numerical difference.
observableIndicators:
  - says 6:4 and 9:7 are equivalent because both differ by 2;
  - chooses mixtures with the same added amount rather than the same scale factor;
  - focuses on "how many more" even when the situation asks "how many times as much".
limitations:
  Produces plausible answers in some contexts but fails under scaling.
```

### State R1 — Paired Numbers Model

```text
stateId: STATE-RATIO-R1
stateKind: PARTIAL_MODEL
description:
  The learner recognizes that two numbers belong together but does not yet treat their relationship as invariant.
observableIndicators:
  - records 3:2 correctly but changes only one side when scaling;
  - can copy a ratio but cannot generate an equivalent ratio;
  - may reverse order without noticing a meaning change.
limitations:
  Ratio is stored as notation rather than a relationship.
```

### State R2 — Procedural Cross-Multiplication Model

```text
stateId: STATE-RATIO-R2
stateKind: PROCEDURAL_MODEL
description:
  The learner can test or solve proportions using a memorized procedure but may not connect the procedure to quantities.
observableIndicators:
  - obtains correct answers through cross multiplication;
  - cannot explain why both quantities must scale together;
  - struggles when data are shown visually or when no equation is provided.
limitations:
  Correct procedure may conceal weak proportional meaning.
```

### State R3 — Multiplicative Comparison Model

```text
stateId: STATE-RATIO-R3
stateKind: CONCEPTUAL_MODEL
description:
  The learner interprets a ratio as an ordered multiplicative comparison and can preserve it through common scaling.
observableIndicators:
  - identifies both quantities and their order;
  - scales both values by the same factor;
  - explains equivalence using groups or multiplicative language;
  - rejects same-difference distractors.
limitations:
  Transfer may still depend on familiar representations or contexts.
```

### State R4 — Connected Proportional Model

```text
stateId: STATE-RATIO-R4
stateKind: CONNECTED_MODEL
description:
  The learner coordinates tables, diagrams, rates, scale, and symbolic forms as representations of one invariant relationship.
observableIndicators:
  - moves between representations;
  - distinguishes proportional from non-proportional situations;
  - chooses efficient reasoning based on the context;
  - validates units and correspondence.
limitations:
  Retention and unfamiliar transfer still require evidence.
```

### State R5 — Transferable Proportional Model

```text
stateId: STATE-RATIO-R5
stateKind: TRANSFERABLE_MODEL
description:
  The learner applies proportional reasoning in unfamiliar world contexts, explains the invariant relationship, and critiques alternatives.
observableIndicators:
  - solves novel mixture, scale, trade, and production situations;
  - creates a proportional model rather than selecting one;
  - identifies hidden non-proportional conditions;
  - explains or mentors another learner using meaningful representations.
```

## 8. Misconception Registry

### M-RATIO-001 — Same Difference Means Same Ratio

The learner treats equal additive differences as equivalent relationships.

**Predictive error:** accepts 3:2 and 5:4 as equivalent because each pair differs by 1.

### M-RATIO-002 — Scale One Side Only

The learner changes one quantity while holding the paired quantity fixed.

**Predictive error:** changes a 3 wood : 2 stone recipe into 6 wood : 2 stone.

### M-RATIO-003 — Order Has No Meaning

The learner treats a:b and b:a as interchangeable.

**Predictive error:** swaps material-to-product and product-to-material rates.

### M-RATIO-004 — Larger Numbers Mean Stronger Ratio

The learner compares absolute numbers rather than relative concentration.

**Predictive error:** claims 8 dye in 20 water is stronger than 5 dye in 10 water because 8 is larger than 5.

### M-RATIO-005 — Every Paired Increase Is Proportional

The learner assumes that two increasing quantities must form a proportional relationship.

**Predictive error:** treats a fixed setup fee plus per-item cost as directly proportional.

### M-RATIO-006 — Procedure Without Quantity Meaning

The learner executes cross multiplication but cannot identify what the result means or whether units are valid.

**Predictive error:** accepts a numerically correct but contextually reversed answer.

Misconceptions are not learner labels. They are hypotheses supported or weakened by evidence patterns.

## 9. Cognitive Transformations

### T-RATIO-001 — Additive Comparison to Multiplicative Comparison

```text
fromStateId: STATE-RATIO-R0
toStateId: STATE-RATIO-R3
transformationIntent:
  Recognize that some comparisons concern relative scale rather than difference.
productiveExperiences:
  - compare two mixtures with the same difference but different concentration;
  - scale grouped objects while preserving composition;
  - contrast "two more" with "twice as much".
requiredContrasts:
  same difference / different ratio;
  different difference / same ratio.
evidenceSignals:
  learner rejects additive distractors and explains the scale factor.
failureSignals:
  learner continues to justify equivalence through subtraction.
```

### T-RATIO-002 — Paired Numbers to Invariant Relationship

```text
fromStateId: STATE-RATIO-R1
toStateId: STATE-RATIO-R3
transformationIntent:
  Treat the pair as one relationship preserved by common scaling.
productiveExperiences:
  - physically duplicate complete material bundles;
  - complete missing rows in a ratio table;
  - repair a build where only one resource was scaled.
requiredContrasts:
  scaling both quantities / scaling one quantity only.
evidenceSignals:
  learner generates equivalent ratios and maintains quantity order.
```

### T-RATIO-003 — Procedure to Meaning

```text
fromStateId: STATE-RATIO-R2
toStateId: STATE-RATIO-R4
transformationIntent:
  Connect symbolic proportion procedures to quantities, representations, and invariant relationships.
productiveExperiences:
  - predict before calculating;
  - explain a symbolic result through grouped materials;
  - choose among table, scale factor, unit rate, or equation methods.
requiredContrasts:
  numerically valid manipulation / contextually meaningful solution.
evidenceSignals:
  learner explains why the method preserves the relationship and validates units.
```

### T-RATIO-004 — Familiar Application to Transfer

```text
fromStateId: STATE-RATIO-R4
toStateId: STATE-RATIO-R5
transformationIntent:
  Recognize proportional structure across unfamiliar representations and world systems.
productiveExperiences:
  - move from recipe to building scale to trade efficiency;
  - identify when a situation is not proportional;
  - construct a model from incomplete world information.
requiredContrasts:
  proportional / affine / unrelated paired change.
evidenceSignals:
  successful novel transfer with explanation and low assistance.
```

## 10. Validation World Scenario

### Mission Candidate: Rebuild the River Bridge Supports

The village engineer has a tested support mixture:

```text
3 crates of timber binder : 2 carts of crushed stone
```

The repaired bridge requires four complete batches. Nearby storage contains several prepared resource combinations, including valid and invalid alternatives.

The player must:

1. identify the two quantities and their order;
2. determine the required scaled quantities;
3. choose or assemble an equivalent mixture;
4. reject a same-difference but non-equivalent mixture;
5. explain or demonstrate why the selected combination preserves strength;
6. later apply the same relationship in a visually different construction task.

The world does not present a detached question screen. The mathematical relationship is embedded in resource preparation, structural consequence, NPC feedback, and later transfer.

## 11. Gameplay Action and Evidence Map

| Player action | Possible evidence | What it does not prove alone |
|---|---|---|
| Selects 12 binder and 8 stone | accuracy; possible common scaling | conceptual reasoning |
| Builds four complete 3:2 bundles | representation; structure awareness | transfer |
| Rejects 6:5 despite equal difference | misconception contrast | retention |
| Explains "both quantities multiplied by four" | reasoning; invariance | independent application |
| Uses a ratio table after a visual model | representation connection | unfamiliar transfer |
| Solves a later recipe or trade task | transfer | delayed retention unless separated in time |
| Completes after direct NPC instruction | assisted success | independent mastery |
| Detects reversed 2:3 order | correspondence and unit awareness | broad proportional competence |

Completion of bridge construction is operational evidence, not automatic mastery evidence.

## 12. Evidence Dimensions

### Accuracy

Was the resulting relationship mathematically valid?

### Consistency

Did the learner preserve the relationship across several decisions rather than succeed once?

### Representation

Can the learner coordinate concrete bundles, tables, diagrams, and symbols?

### Reasoning

Can the learner explain common scaling and reject additive reasoning?

### Contrast

Can the learner distinguish valid proportional relationships from plausible non-examples?

### Transfer

Can the learner recognize and use the same structure in a new world context?

### Assistance

How much prompting, correction, or direct instruction was required?

### Retention

Does the understanding remain available after time and intervening activity?

### Confidence Calibration

Does the learner's expressed confidence align with the quality of reasoning and evidence?

No single dimension is sufficient for a transferable mastery decision.

## 14. Mission Projection Variants

The same target may generate different missions while preserving learning authority.

### Construction Projection

Scale material bundles or building dimensions while preserving a design relationship.

### Mixture Projection

Maintain composition while changing total production quantity.

### Trade Projection

Compare exchange relationships and detect misleading offers.

### Farming Projection

Scale feed, seed, water, or planting ratios across plots.

### Workshop Projection

Compare production rates and allocate worker-machine capacity.

### NPC Support Projection

Diagnose an NPC's mistaken additive or reversed-ratio reasoning.

These systems provide context and actions. They may not redefine the target, evidence requirements, or mastery outcome.

## 15. Readiness Implications for Phase 16B

This slice reveals that readiness cannot be represented by a single percentage.

A learner may be:

- ready conceptually but weak in notation;
- procedurally successful but conceptually unready;
- strong in familiar mixtures but weak in transfer;
- blocked by multiplication facts rather than ratio meaning;
- affected by quantity-order or unit confusion;
- able to solve but unable to detect non-proportional situations.

Phase 16B therefore needs a learner-specific state projection containing at least:

```text
LearnerTargetReadiness
- targetId
- hypothesizedCognitiveStateId
- prerequisiteReadiness
- misconceptionHypotheses
- evidenceCoverageByDimension
- representationReadiness
- assistanceDependency
- transferStatus
- retentionStatus
- confidence
- unresolvedContradictions
- nextUsefulTransformationIds
```

The readiness engine must report hypotheses and evidence confidence, not claim direct access to a learner's mind.

## 16. Mission Generation Implications for Phase 16C

Mission generation must select a transformation, not merely a topic.

A conforming mission candidate should be derived from:

```text
Learning Target
        ↓
Hypothesized Current Cognitive State
        ↓
Selected Cognitive Transformation
        ↓
Required Contrast and Evidence Gap
        ↓
Suitable World Affordance
        ↓
Mission Candidate
```

Examples:

- `T-RATIO-001` should generate a contrast between same-difference and same-ratio situations;
- `T-RATIO-002` should require scaling complete paired bundles;
- `T-RATIO-003` should connect a symbolic method to quantity meaning;
- `T-RATIO-004` should move the learner into a structurally similar but unfamiliar context.

Repeating the same numerical exercise with new values does not necessarily create a new cognitive transformation.

## 17. Mastery Interpretation

This validation slice supports the following progression interpretation:

```text
UNKNOWN
  No reliable evidence.

INTRODUCED
  Learner has encountered the relationship and representations.

PRACTICING
  Emerging success with inconsistency or assistance.

CONSISTENT
  Stable success in familiar contexts with adequate reasoning.

MASTERED
  Conceptual relationship established across required representations and contrasts.

TRANSFERABLE
  Understanding transfers to unfamiliar contexts and remains after delay.

MENTOR
  Learner can diagnose and support another learner without replacing their reasoning.
```

A mission completion event may contribute evidence but may not directly assign any of these states.

## 18. Validation Findings

### Finding 1 — Topic Nodes Are Insufficient

`Ratio` alone cannot represent the difference between additive reasoning, paired-number reasoning, procedure-only success, and transferable proportional understanding.

### Finding 2 — Misconceptions Must Be First-Class

Predictable wrong models are necessary for readiness diagnosis and mission contrast generation.

### Finding 3 — Transformation Requires Contrast

A productive activity is not enough. The graph must state which contrasting cases reveal the intended change in reasoning.

### Finding 4 — Representation Is Part of Understanding

A learner may succeed symbolically but fail with concrete quantities, or the reverse. Representation coverage must remain explicit.

### Finding 5 — World Activity Can Produce Rich Evidence

Builder's Valley can gather meaningful evidence through material selection, grouping, repair, explanation, rejection of alternatives, and later transfer without pausing the game for a detached quiz.

### Finding 6 — Operational Success and Learning Success Must Remain Separate

A bridge may be completed through guessing, copying, NPC assistance, or conceptual reasoning. The world state and learning state therefore require separate authorities.

### Finding 7 — Readiness Is a Structured Hypothesis

Phase 16B must infer the most supported current model from evidence and preserve uncertainty or contradiction.

## 19. Required Refinements to the 16A Contract

The parent 16A architecture remains valid. This slice adds the following clarifications for downstream design:

1. `CognitiveTransformation.requiredContrasts` is mandatory for transformations intended to resolve a known misconception.
2. Learning targets must declare minimum representation coverage rather than listing representations as descriptive metadata only.
3. Misconception evidence must support both confirming and disconfirming signals.
4. World projections must declare which evidence dimensions they can and cannot produce.
5. Mastery expectations must include transfer and retention requirements where the target claims durable understanding.
6. Learner state projections must preserve uncertainty and conflicting evidence.
7. Operational completion must never be treated as implicit learning completion.

These are contract clarifications, not evidence that the foundational model failed.
