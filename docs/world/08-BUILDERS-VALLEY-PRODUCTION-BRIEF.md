# 08 — Builder's Valley Production Brief

## Status

Production target definition v1.

## Mission

Builder's Valley must prove that Math Learning World can become a place children want to revisit, not merely a level that delivers tasks.

The environment should communicate one idea before any text appears:

> The world can be understood, repaired, and improved through careful creation.

## Emotional promise

The player should feel:

- safe enough to experiment;
- curious about how the valley works;
- proud that their actions leave visible improvement;
- drawn toward the bridge, river, waterfall, and workshop;
- aware that the place existed before them and will continue after them.

## Production Hero Frame

The approved target frame must show one coherent composition containing:

- a readable river corridor;
- a waterfall that establishes depth and direction;
- Bridge Crossing as the primary landmark;
- a workshop terrace as the center of human craft;
- sculpted terrain that frames movement;
- vegetation arranged in masses, not scattered decoration;
- warm directional light and cool environmental contrast;
- clear foreground, middle ground, and background layers;
- at least one visible invitation to explore beyond the immediate task.

The Hero Frame is a production target. Runtime captures must be reviewed against its composition, hierarchy, value structure, color relationships, and emotional effect.

## Scene hierarchy

### Primary landmark — Bridge Crossing

Meaning: connection through structure.

The bridge must be recognizable from a distance and remain visually dominant without becoming oversized. Its structure should make forces, spans, repetition, support, and proportion visible.

### Secondary landmark — Workshop Terrace

Meaning: understanding becomes craft.

The workshop should feel used and useful. It is not a generic prop cluster. Storage, tools, materials, staging areas, and incomplete work should communicate an understandable workflow.

### Natural anchor — Waterfall and river

Meaning: systems flow through the world.

Water establishes route, elevation, sound, movement, and continuity with regions beyond the valley.

### Supporting masses

- terrain shelves;
- tree groups;
- rock formations;
- path edges;
- work areas;
- distant mountain or forest silhouettes.

Supporting elements must frame the primary read rather than compete with it.

## Player approach sequence

```text
Arrival overlook
→ first view of bridge and waterfall
→ descent toward workshop terrace
→ close encounter with materials and tools
→ approach to structural problem
→ interaction and revision
→ bridge completion or restoration
→ crossing reveals the next horizon
```

The player should understand where to go through composition, light, motion, sound, and path shape before relying on markers.

## Gameplay-safe composition

The scene must preserve:

- clear walkable surfaces;
- strong contrast between navigable and non-navigable areas;
- safe camera distance around interaction zones;
- no vegetation hiding critical objects;
- no decorative clutter inside placement or pickup zones;
- stable silhouettes at mobile resolution;
- readable tool, material, and construction states;
- collision that matches visible forms closely enough to maintain trust.

## Core player verbs

- approach;
- observe;
- gather;
- select;
- carry;
- place;
- remove;
- compare;
- repair;
- cross;
- return.

The production environment must support these verbs naturally. Art should reinforce the loop rather than become a separate layer.

## Mathematical language made visible

Builder's Valley should expose mathematical relationships through the environment:

- quantity through material requirements;
- length through spans and spacing;
- area through work surfaces and placement zones;
- symmetry and repetition through bridge structure;
- comparison through alternative materials or arrangements;
- sequence through construction order;
- balance and constraint through structural feedback;
- estimation through distance, count, and fit.

Formal symbols should appear only after the physical relationship has meaning.

## Before-and-after state

Before:

- route interrupted or structurally incomplete;
- workshop active but constrained;
- materials present without final organization;
- opposite bank visible but not fully accessible.

After:

- bridge reads as complete and trustworthy;
- travel and community movement improve;
- workshop reflects the player's contribution;
- new view or route becomes available;
- the valley retains evidence of the player's work.

The transformation must be visible from multiple distances, including the original arrival view.

## Art language

### Shape

- broad, friendly masses;
- rounded terrain transitions;
- readable hand-built geometry;
- controlled asymmetry;
- repeated structural rhythms where mathematically meaningful.

### Materials

- warm timber;
- grounded stone;
- soft soil and grass transitions;
- clear, lively water;
- restrained metal accents;
- visible wear where it explains use.

### Color

- warm craft and settlement tones;
- cool water and depth tones;
- vegetation used to frame, not overwhelm;
- saturated accents reserved for interaction, life, or discovery.

### Lighting

- warm directional key light;
- soft readable shadows;
- atmosphere that separates depth layers;
- no crushed dark interaction zones;
- waterfall and river highlights used as movement cues.

## Audio intent

Audio should establish the valley before music explains emotion:

- directional water;
- workshop rhythm;
- material handling;
- soft wildlife and wind layers;
- structural sounds that make placement and stability understandable.

Music should support curiosity and calm competence, not urgency.

## Runtime production layers

1. greybox and camera proof;
2. terrain and path blockout;
3. water corridor proof;
4. bridge landmark blockout;
5. workshop workflow blockout;
6. gameplay collision and interaction zones;
7. material and vegetation pass;
8. lighting and atmosphere pass;
9. prop storytelling pass;
10. mobile readability and performance pass;
11. Hero Frame comparison captures;
12. human play validation.

No later layer should conceal an unresolved problem in an earlier layer.

## Acceptance criteria

The slice is visually accepted only when:

- a first-time viewer identifies the bridge as the main landmark within seconds;
- the route from arrival to workshop and bridge is readable without constant UI guidance;
- foreground, middle ground, and background remain distinct;
- the runtime image carries the same composition logic as the Hero Frame;
- vegetation appears composed rather than randomly distributed;
- the environment looks intentional from the principal gameplay camera;
- mobile presentation preserves landmark and interaction readability.

The slice is experientially accepted only when:

- the player can complete the gather-place-revise loop without unnecessary tool friction;
- mistakes provide visible structural information;
- the completed bridge changes access and meaning;
- the experience remains enjoyable without points, badges, or a question overlay;
- at least one mathematical relationship transfers into a changed situation.

## Explicit exclusions

This slice does not require:

- the full world map;
- all landmark regions;
- broad NPC simulation;
- final economy;
- complete curriculum coverage;
- extensive HUD redesign;
- unrelated gameplay systems;
- high-density micro-props before composition is proven.

## Production decision

The next implementation work should improve the complete scene in composition order. It should not return to isolated asset accumulation or feature expansion until this brief's acceptance evidence is available.
