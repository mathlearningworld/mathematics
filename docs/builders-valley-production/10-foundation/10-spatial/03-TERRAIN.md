# Builders Valley Terrain Standard

Status: Active Foundation Standard  
Authority: Builders Valley Production Bible

## 1. Purpose

Terrain is the first storyteller and the primary spatial structure of Builders Valley. It creates routes, boundaries, destinations, risk, safety, landmark purpose, and visual depth before structures or decoration are added.

Terrain must be designed as connected geographic mass, not accumulated from independent visual patches.

## 2. Core terrain doctrine

Terrain creates purpose.

- A bridge exists because a meaningful terrain cut requires crossing.
- A mine exists because the land exposes or contains an entrance.
- A terrace exists because elevation organizes activity.
- A forest edge exists because vegetation and ground mass define a boundary.
- A path exists because it connects meaningful places through geography.

Structures must respond to terrain. Terrain must not be shaped merely to justify assets that were already placed.

## 3. Terrain construction order

Every landmark terrain design follows this order:

1. Base elevation field.
2. Primary terrain masses.
3. Cuts and voids such as river, canyon, ravine, or basin.
4. Traversable surfaces and route connections.
5. Cliff faces, slopes, banks, and transitions.
6. Ground material regions.
7. Vegetation boundaries and environmental storytelling.
8. Structures and props.

Later layers may refine earlier layers but must not conceal an unresolved terrain problem.

## 4. Terrain mass

A terrain mass is a large geographic form that changes silhouette, traversal, framing, elevation, or world understanding.

Examples:

- plateau;
- ridge;
- cliff wall;
- canyon side;
- raised terrace;
- river bank;
- distant mountain mass.

Non-examples:

- isolated decorative rock;
- crate;
- tree instance;
- fence;
- workshop prop.

Terrain masses must be judged at hero-frame scale before surface detail is added.

## 5. Organic silhouette rule

Organic does not mean random. Terrain contours must combine authored rhythm, asymmetry, and readable structure.

Required qualities:

- avoid long unbroken rectangular walls;
- vary contour direction and depth deliberately;
- use major, secondary, and minor shape scales;
- preserve clear route openings;
- avoid noisy edge variation that destroys landmark readability;
- allow silhouettes to remain readable at gameplay zoom.

## 6. Rivers and terrain cuts

A river, canyon, or ravine is a spatial system, not a colored strip.

It must define:

- source and continuation direction;
- banks or cliff boundaries;
- width rhythm;
- crossing logic;
- elevation relationship;
- visual flow;
- traversal prohibition or constraint;
- relationship to the hero frame.

A cut fails when it appears as a rectangle inserted beneath a bridge or when its banks do not belong to the surrounding terrain.

## 7. Cliffs and elevation

Cliffs must communicate height through silhouette, face treatment, top-edge separation, cast shadow or value change, and relationship to traversable ground.

A cliff must not read as a freestanding gray wall.

Required separation:

- cliff top belongs to the upper terrain surface;
- cliff face expresses the elevation transition;
- cliff base connects to lower ground, water, or inaccessible void;
- material and lighting reinforce, but do not invent, the form.

## 8. Traversal and safety

Terrain must make playable and non-playable areas legible through form before collision prevents movement.

Use:

- elevation;
- edge shape;
- path continuity;
- material transition;
- vegetation boundary;
- structure placement;
- lighting hierarchy.

Do not rely exclusively on invisible collision or warning text.

## 9. Terrain material regions

Ground materials follow geographic and gameplay logic.

Examples:

- compacted earth on repeated travel routes;
- grass on stable open ground;
- exposed rock on steep faces;
- wet or darker soil near water;
- constructed surfaces at workshops and settlements.

Material borders must support terrain form. They must not be arbitrary patches used to create visual activity.

## 10. Terrain ownership

One authoritative terrain system owns:

- elevation field;
- primary masses;
- terrain cuts;
- traversable ground envelope;
- major material regions;
- terrain collision contract.

Vegetation, structures, effects, and landmark renderers may consume terrain data but must not independently create competing primary terrain.

Temporary migration adapters must declare their limits and removal condition.

## 11. Landmark terrain contract

Every landmark must specify:

- geographic purpose;
- primary mass;
- primary cut or elevation event;
- traversable route;
- blocked boundaries;
- landmark anchor surfaces;
- foreground, midground, and background terrain roles;
- terrain owner;
- acceptance screenshot.

## 12. Acceptance criteria

Terrain passes when:

- the landmark's purpose is understandable before structures are added;
- the primary route follows geographic logic;
- terrain silhouette reads clearly at hero-frame scale;
- cliffs, banks, and cuts connect coherently;
- no old and new primary terrain systems visibly overlap;
- logical grid boundaries are not unintentionally visible;
- materials reinforce existing form;
- one runtime owner controls primary terrain;
- gameplay boundaries are visually legible.

## 13. Failure conditions

Terrain fails when:

- a bridge floats over a decorative water rectangle;
- cliffs read as boxes or walls detached from land;
- paths conflict or lead nowhere;
- added detail attempts to hide weak massing;
- multiple patches produce duplicate banks, roads, or ground planes;
- vegetation is used to conceal unresolved terrain seams;
- collision and visible ground disagree.
